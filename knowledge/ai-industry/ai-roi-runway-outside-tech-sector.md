# The AI ROI runway could be long outside the tech sector (Torsten Slok / Apollo)

Torsten Slok (Partner and Chief Economist, Apollo Global Management), June 30
2026: AI valuations face repricing risk because they assume rapid earnings growth
from margin expansion that isn't materializing outside tech.

## Core argument

- **No margin signal yet.** *"So far there are no signs of profit margins rising
  outside the tech sector,"* undermining the assumption behind current AI-linked
  valuations.
- **Adoption speed is uneven.** Tech firms integrate AI "overnight"; most of the
  economy faces structural friction — *"deep process re-engineering and data
  governance requirements could delay structural productivity gains well beyond
  what the market currently projects."*
- **Sectors named as slow-moving:** healthcare, banking, insurance, energy, pharma,
  manufacturing, transportation, construction, legal, government.
- **The risk:** *"A mismatch between current earnings expectations and the actual
  time firms need to generate ROI on AI investments could have significant
  implications for many AI company valuations today."*

## Hacker News discussion (counterpoints and reality checks)

The linked HN thread ([item 48810533](https://news.ycombinator.com/item?id=48810533))
raised substantive pushback worth recording alongside the thesis:

- **Against:** efficiency gains don't have to raise aggregate margins if
  competitors erode the advantage via pricing (a "red-queen's race"); an app
  explosion may already be happening invisibly, in internal tools and niche
  projects rather than visible commercial products; prior general-purpose
  technologies (IT in the 1980s) took decades to show up in productivity
  statistics.
- **Supporting the caution:** token costs have been rising, not converging to
  zero; building a profitable application is far harder than prompting a model;
  historically, firms tend to pocket efficiency gains rather than pass them to
  customers.
- **Net read from the thread:** non-tech AI adoption is genuinely early, and a
  fair ROI assessment may need another 1–2 years of data.
